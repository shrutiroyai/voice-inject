Building a Local AI Meeting Transcriber
That Learns Who's Speaking

How I built a fully local, privacy-first voice transcription system that gets smarter over time — and what I learned about the surprisingly hard problem of speaker recognition.

The Problem

Why I Built This

I take a lot of meetings. I write a lot of documents. I'm constantly coding. And I realized something: my thoughts move faster than my fingers. The bottleneck in my workflow isn't thinking — it's the physical act of typing.

Here's the math that changed my perspective:

We speak 3-4x faster than we type. That's not a marginal improvement — it's a fundamentally different velocity of output. When I'm drafting a design doc, explaining a code review, writing Slack messages, or commenting on a PR, typing is the bottleneck. Voice removes it.

I set out to solve two distinct problems:

1

Meeting Transcription

Automatically capture who said what in every meeting — with speaker names, timestamps, and searchable text. No more "let me check my notes" or "can someone share the action items?"

2

Voice-to-Text Anywhere

Double-tap a key and speak. The transcribed text appears wherever my cursor is — IDE, Slack, docs, email, terminal. Any app, any surface. 150 WPM instead of 40.

I looked at existing solutions: Otter.ai, Fireflies, Whisper-based cloud services. They all had the same problem — your data leaves your machine. For enterprise developers, that's a non-starter. Meeting discussions about architecture, internal systems, and strategy can't be piped to a third-party server. And none of them offered the "speak anywhere, paste anywhere" workflow I wanted.

So I set out to build something that stays entirely on your laptop. No cloud APIs. No data exfiltration. Just local AI models doing the heavy lifting on your own hardware — and a universal dictation mode that works across every app on your system.

The Hard Problem

Speaker Identification: Harder Than You Think

Transcription is largely solved. Whisper handles it beautifully. But knowing who said what across multiple meetings? That's where things get interesting.

The naive approach seems simple: extract a voice embedding (a 512-dimensional vector that represents someone's vocal characteristics), compare it to stored embeddings using cosine similarity, and match if the score is high enough. In theory, same-speaker pairs should give scores near 1.0 and different speakers near 0.

Reality Check

Here's what I actually observed:

0.35
Same Speaker
(different segments)
0.61
Same Speaker
(with centroid)
0.12
Different
Speakers

Same-speaker similarity of 0.3–0.4? That's barely above noise. Setting a threshold of 0.85 (what you'd expect to need) means nothing ever matches. The embeddings are simply too noisy for short meeting segments.

Why Embeddings Are Noisy

1

Short Segments

Meeting turns are often 1-3 seconds. The embedding model needs longer audio for stable voiceprints. Under 1.5 seconds, outputs are near-random.

2

System Audio Quality

Capturing meeting audio from speakers (not microphone) adds compression artifacts, background noise, and volume variation.

3

Speaking Variation

Same person speaking casually vs. presenting vs. laughing produces wildly different embeddings. Tone shifts through a meeting are normal.

4

Model Limitations

pyannote/embedding was trained for longer-form speaker verification, not real-time meeting diarization with 2-second clips.

The Solutions

How I Made It Actually Work

Each problem required a different insight. Here's the progression from "nothing matches" to "it recognizes me reliably":

1

L2 Normalization

Raw embeddings had wildly different norms (640–1500). While cosine similarity is theoretically norm-independent, normalizing to unit length improved numerical stability significantly.

2

Centroid-Based Matching

Instead of comparing a new embedding against each stored embedding individually (max-of-N), I compute the mean centroid of all stored embeddings per speaker. Averaging smooths out per-segment noise. Same-speaker similarity jumped from 0.35 → 0.61 for longer segments.

# Instead of: max(similarity(query, each_stored_embedding))
# We do:     similarity(query, mean(all_stored_embeddings))

centroid = stored_embeddings.mean(axis=0)
centroid = centroid / np.linalg.norm(centroid)  # re-normalize
similarity = 1 - cosine_distance(query, centroid)
3

Rolling Centroids with EMA

During a session, each confirmed speaker match updates their centroid using an exponential moving average. Early in a session, the centroid adapts quickly. As it stabilizes (more matches), new samples have less influence. The system literally adapts to how someone sounds today.

# Exponential moving average update
count = speaker_match_count[name]
alpha = 1.0 / (count + 1)  # decreasing influence over time
new_centroid = (1 - alpha) * centroid + alpha * new_embedding
centroid = new_centroid / np.linalg.norm(new_centroid)
4

Minimum Duration Thresholds

Segments under 1.5 seconds produce garbage embeddings. I skip embedding extraction entirely for short clips — they'd only poison the centroid. For enrollment (adding a new speaker), the bar is even higher: 3 seconds minimum, or concatenate multiple clips.

5

Concatenated Enrollment

When you annotate a speaker, ALL their segments in that session are stitched together into one long clip. A single high-quality embedding from 10+ seconds of combined audio is far more reliable than five embeddings from 2-second snippets.

6

Accumulation Over Time (up to 15 embeddings)

Each session you annotate adds a new embedding to the speaker's profile. Over time, the centroid incorporates samples from different days, different background noise levels, different moods. More diversity = more robust matching. Oldest embeddings are dropped when the cap is reached.

7

Deferred Identification

The key insight: diarization and identification are different problems. Diarization (detecting speaker changes) is fast and runs live. Identification (matching who) is expensive and can wait. I run diarization in real-time for the live transcript, and defer identification to annotation time. Result: zero-latency live transcription.

The aha moment

Separation between speakers (different people: ~0.12 similarity) is excellent — even when within-speaker consistency is poor. This means a low threshold (0.35) gives reliable identification with minimal false positives. The model is great at distinguishing voices — it's just not great at producing consistent absolute scores.

Under the Hood

Technical Deep Dive

The VAD Loop

The core recording loop uses WebRTC's Voice Activity Detection to segment audio in real-time. Every 30ms frame is classified as speech or silence. After 400ms of consecutive silence (configurable), the accumulated audio is cut and sent for transcription.

# Simplified VAD loop
while recording:
    frame = get_audio_frame(30ms)

    if vad.is_speech(frame):
        silence_count = 0
        has_speech = True
    else:
        silence_count += 1

    if has_speech and silence_count >= threshold:
        segment = drain_buffer()
        transcribe_async(segment)  # non-blocking

Speaker Diarization vs. Identification

These are often conflated but they're fundamentally different operations:

Diarization Identification
Question "Did the speaker change?" "Who is this speaker?"
Scope Within a single audio segment Across all sessions ever
Speed Fast (segmentation model) Slow (embedding + DB lookup)
When it runs Live, during recording Deferred, at annotation time
Output "Speaker 1", "Speaker 2" "Shruti", "Tarunay"

The Annotation Flow

When a user assigns a name in the UI:

1. User clicks "Assign" on a segment labeled "Speaker 1"
2. All segments with label "Speaker 1" → renamed to "Shruti"
3. ALL audio from "Shruti" segments is concatenated
4. Single embedding extracted from combined audio (3s+ minimum)
5. Embedding stored in speaker DB (accumulates up to 15 per person)
6. Remaining "Speaker N" segments re-identified against updated DB
7. Live transcript updated via WebSocket (names appear instantly)
8. .txt file regenerated with correct names

The Speaker Database

Deliberately simple — a JSON file mapping names to lists of 512-dimensional embeddings:

{
  "Shruti": [
    [0.0234, -0.0891, 0.0456, ...],  // embedding from session 1
    [0.0198, -0.0923, 0.0501, ...],  // embedding from session 2
    ...                               // up to 15 embeddings
  ],
  "Tarunay": [
    [0.0567, -0.0234, 0.0891, ...]
  ]
}

Matching computes the centroid (mean) of all stored embeddings per speaker, then measures cosine similarity against the query embedding. No neural network at query time — just linear algebra.

Get Started

Installation

git clone https://github.com/shrutiroyai/voice-inject.git
cd voice-inject
bash install.sh

That's it. The install script sets up the Python environment, downloads the ML models, and starts both the server and client. Open http://localhost:3000 in your browser.
